The thing you should actually know — and it is not about you

And the thing you should actually know, which is not about you at all: The most accessible book on the roster is accessible because its author had given up on philosophy's technical apparatus — and he says so twice, in writing, to himself. Stoicism had three parts: logic, physics, ethics. Marcus abandoned two of them. In Book I he thanks the gods that "I did not waste my time on writers [of histories], or in the resolution of syllogisms, or occupy myself about the investigation of appearances in the heavens" (I.17) — and at VII.67, in a passage nobody quotes, he is consoling himself for a FAILURE: "And because thou hast despaired of becoming a dialectician and skilled in the knowledge of nature, do not for this reason renounce the hope of being both free and modest, and social and obedient to God." That is not modesty; it is a man talking himself down off a competence he could not reach. So the reason an untrained person can open the Meditations and read it is not that the book was simplified for them. It is that the emperor was standing on their side of the gate. He is the only philosopher on this roster who is, by his own written confession, an amateur — and the gate dimensions are near zero because HE COULD NOT PASS THEM EITHER.
